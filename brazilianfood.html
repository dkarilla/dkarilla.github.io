Brazilian Food

As a dual citizen of Brazil, I was raised around many Brazilian customs and traditions. I have traveled around Brazil with my mother since childhood. I have been Rio de Janeiro, São Paulo, Brasilia and many rural areas. Unfortunately, I am not fluent in Portuguese, the national language of Brazil. I hope to spend more time in Brazil following the completion of my education and immerse myself in the language and culture until I can speak Portuguese like a native.

My favorite part of Brazilian culture is the cuisine. I grew up eating rice and beans for dinner every day. I still make rice and beans pretty frequently even now that I live away from home. Rice and beans are two very large staples in the Brazilian diet; however, the cuisine offered around Brazil is still diverse. According to Brazil.org, the various types of dishes and ingredients used in Brazil are based largely on geographical location.

Fruits are that grow in tropical or sub-tropical climates are especially common in Brazil. I remember climbing mango trees when I was younger. My mother would grow papaya and guava on our family farm. Passion fruit, pineapple, oranges and the lesser-known jabuticaba are all also very common fruits and are used to make fresh juices or desserts.

As for vegetables, root vegetables are used often. For example, yucca is a popular root vegetable and yucca flour serves as the basis for many breads or baked good, including a type of cheese biscuit called pão de queijo.

Brazilians do not have a lot of appetizers because of how filling rice and beans can be as a main course. There is a lot of street food sold by vendors or in small stores. Coxinha, a fried food filled with shredded chicken, is my personal favorite. Bolinhos are also popular and are deep-friend balls made of rice-based batter.
