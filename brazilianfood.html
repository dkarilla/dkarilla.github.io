Brazilian Food

As a dual citizen of Brazil, I was raised around many Brazilian customs and traditions. I have traveled around Brazil with my mother since childhood. I have been Rio de Janeiro, São Paulo, Brasilia and many rural areas. Unfortunately, I am not fluent in Portuguese, the national language of Brazil. I hope to spend more time in Brazil following the completion of my education and immerse myself in the language and culture until I can speak Portuguese like a native.

My favorite part of Brazilian culture is the cuisine. I grew up eating rice and beans for dinner every day. I still make rice and beans pretty frequently even now that I live away from home. Rice and beans are two very large staples in the Brazilian diet; however, the cuisine offered around Brazil is still diverse. According to Brazil.org, the various types of dishes and ingredients used in Brazil are based largely on geographical location.

Fruits are that grow in tropical or sub-tropical climates are especially common in Brazil. I remember climbing mango trees when I was younger. My mother would grow papaya and guava on our family farm. Passion fruit, pineapple, oranges and the lesser-known jabuticaba are all also very common fruits and are used to make fresh juices or desserts.

According to a Princeton website, the Europeans immigrants from Portugal, Germany and Italy introduced wine, leaf vegetables and dairy products into Brazilian cuisine. Root vegetables are used often. For example, yucca or manioc is a popular root vegetable and yucca flour serves as the basis for many breads or baked good, including a type of cheese biscuit called pão de queijo. When the potatoes were not available to immigrants, they substituted with the native root manioc.

Brazilians do not have a lot of appetizers because of how filling rice and beans can be as a main course. There is a lot of street food sold by vendors or in small stores. Coxinha, a fried food filled with shredded chicken, is my personal favorite. Coxinha is more common in Southeast Brazil, where my mother is from. Bolinhos are also popular and are deep-fried balls made of rice-based batter.

As stated on gourmet.com, foods that come directly from northern Brazil include the acai berry and peppers. Acai has become popular in the United States, largely considered a “superfood” that is being used in many smoothie and yogurt recipes. The chili peppers used to create pimenta, or hot sauce, grow in northern Brazil.

Some miscellaneous Brazilian foods include requejão, a type of cream cheese served at breakfast or as a snack. Romeo e Julieta is a bizarre flavor combination made up of a slice of cheese and a slice of guava paste. Brazilians do tend to drink a lot of fruit juice; however, one of their signature beverages is Guaraná, a soda made from the Brazilian fruit of the same name.
